Where Arts & Technology Collide

I will always say that I would not be able to pursue computer science without some creative or art aspect mixed with it. The stereotype is that students who study computer science obsess over technology and lack diverse interests, which is the complete opposite of who I am. Whenever I was asked for my favorite subject, I would usually answer with four or more of them, as I could never choose one and leave out the others. In addition to computer science and music, I love Spanish, especially when it integrates with music. I listen to Spanish-speaking music all the time. I also love creative writing, and it really came from the early years of high school and became a part of who I am. In retrospect, I'm surprised I did not foresee myself integrating music and writing, as it seems like an obvious path. I guess I thought, since I was pursuing computer science and music, that I would not have the time or concentration to utilize the other subjects I value so much. However, as I went through college having not written anything for me outside of classes, I realized how I desperately I had been wanting to write, and it caused this surge of desire to write songs in 2023.

My Efforts at Northeastern

While I was on co-op in New York, bouncing between my apartment in Boston and my house in New York (my internship was hybrid), I had realized that my time at Northeastern really was coming to a close, and I wanted to leave my mark in the university with the idea of an umbrella organization for all the arts and technology clubs on campus. The Interdisciplinary Arts & Technology Council was proposed and spearheaded by me throughout the rest of 2024. A lot of the issues and missed opportunities I felt were present between these interdisciplinary clubs aligned almost one to one with what was decribed in our reading on interdisciplinarity, A Time for Interdisciplinarity.

"I agree with the criticism that university departments hold back interdisciplinary development. One of the main struggles in developing the IATC (Interdisciplinary Arts & Technology Council) is that most organizations at Northeastern need to be under a single college (or center/overseer). After my discussions with student government and others, I have concluded that this umbrella organization would require support from more than just faculty of a single college (which creates more hurdles in the process). Although Northeastern already has interdisciplinarity incorporated into its academics (e.g. combined majors and programs), the concept of facilitating collaborations and networking between clubs (i.e. outside classes), especially those who belong to different colleges, continues to be missed potential, blocked by the inherent divide of disciplines by the university."

— Me

I was unfortunately not able to get the IATC into fruition, mainly due to scheduling issues. However, I hope that the faculty in the music department can one day pass down the torch to someone else with the same drive and passion as me...

In that same post, I discussed how incredibly interdisciplinary music technology is, but I also mentioned how I tried pushing for a more integrative curriculum for my major, and how there were still hurdles that I needed to overcome. Combined majors at Northeastern are at times inflexible, and I mentioned how in the industries that have music technology, it's the complete opposite.

I think the course curriculum changes I proposed are going through a process right now, so hopefully they are eventually approved. Along with the IATC, this was also my way of leaving a mark on the university.

The Power of Interdisciplinarity

"I want to reaffirm my perspective on interdisciplinarity. Even at lower levels of education, disciplines intersect all time. Physics and calculus go hand & hand, social studies often relies on English skills, and digital art has become more prominent over the years, requiring both artistic and technical skills.

Nearly all my classes were interdisciplinary. For my composition classes, I had to use my expertise in physics and engineering to recreate real-life instruments using synthesizers. Right now, I am taking a class in digital audio signal processing (DSP), programming in C to perform operations on audio signals (requiring an understanding of computer science, physics, and digital audio). I have taken game-focused CS classes, allowing me to actually incorporate game audio, design principles, and game development/industry standards.

I think it's less about whether or not disciplines should cross more often (since they inherently do) and more about how interdisciplinary education is taught."

— Me

Interdisciplinarity has always been important to me (clearly). It's what allows for diverse perspectives, and it's where passions intersect. While having strong focuses on specific disciplines is understandable, I've noticed that it's not always enough for students to merely pursue, for example, a degree in computer science. Nowadays, some companies want specific backgrounds. Working at a medical sensor company may require signal processing. Game companies often require game design knowledge in addition to software development principles. This is of course not always the case, but the more competitive the industry gets, the more specialized and interdisciplinary they want their employees to be. While I don't completely agree with companies trying to find the perfect candidate that doesn't exist, but I don't think specializing in a particular interdiscipline is a bad idea. At a certain point an interdiscipline becomes a whole discipline itself, like computer science or music technology.

I thought this was interesting to look back upon, since I was essentially making an argument and believed strongly in it. Me giving the various examples of disciplines intersecting illustrates how much I recognize interdisciplinarity and how much I think and care about it. I value being able to voice a perspective, provide examples, and contribute to good conversations. Especially in the era of the internet, data and sources or more important than ever.

"...This perspective was shaped early on by my experience in a Montessori school, where learning was inherently interdisciplinary. Subjects weren’t taught in isolation but as interconnected concepts. Instead of learning math, science, and history separately, we explored overarching themes that blended them together..."

— Chandler

Before reading Chandler's post, I had no idea Montessori schools were a thing, and I was really intrigued to see how one would be in person. It made me think about how a student's strength in one subject could help them be stronger in other subjects that they would perhaps otherwise dislike. It could expose fields and interdisciplines that students have never heard of before, increasing their interest in the disciplines they entail. Personally, social studies became way more interesting once I realized how important and relevant it is. I assume this would be similar to how a student would feel if they greatly dislike a subject at first. I think people would be more interested in learning imaginary numbers if they know how it was used in sound engineering (or maybe that's just me)...

"I think it is also important to note how many disciplines have been 'clashing,' particularly art vs. tech fields. I would say it's essentially a battle between depth and efficiency. The large focus on efficiency in our society today has caused various problems, especially recently. Most notably:

  • There is little consideration for the (financial and moral) consequences; 'pursuing a goal despite core issues that could arise'
  • The loss of quality and diversity, especially due to the lack of collaboration (or compromise) between art and technology
  • The lack of creativity, which actually undermines innovation (which is the complete opposite of what we generally want)"

— Me

I thought this was interesting since I think in my midterm reflection I had said I didn't pushed for social justice at that point. Looking back to this, as well as my reply to Nora's reply this post, I was very much trying to address a social issue here. My points here addressed how lacking diverse perspectives and disciplines lead to ethical issues, an over-prioritization of efficiency, and less innovation. There are very serious consequences of constantly pushing for efficiency, especially with tech undermining art, and I think some of the most well-known companies that are doing this are starting to slowly recognize it...

Perhaps interdisciplinarity and social justice go hand in hand.
