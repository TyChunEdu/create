We All Deserve a Voice

2025 has been a rough year, and I hate to say it, but there's not really any signs of it getting better any time soon. However, what has been an incredible help this semester is my discussions with my peers and even my professors. Our discussions on disciplines' impact on social justice were well-timed, and I greatly enjoyed reflecting upon music's role in advocating for change, throughout history and possibly by me in the future...

An Escape to a Distorted Reality

The core idea I was illustrating is that people absorbing certain media sources are doing so to escape from the scary ever-evolving world, causing their reality to be distorted and be opposed to any kind of progressive change.

"...a lot of people prioritize what they learn from media over their own realities..."

— Me

As I discussed in the post, while I added sources to some of the claims I was making, people tend to not really care about research and credibility (which is scary and sad). I decided to take a different approach and essentially logic my way through my reasoning in a way that is effective and understandable. I essentially provided strong straight-forward examples. The goal with talking to people with beliefs that harm others (and themselves) is to get people to at least be introduced to the conversation. Sometimes it just takes one person to put two and two together, especially with "evidence" they can't deny since it's actually how they experience the world (most of the time). It is an issue most of us cannot avoid, since these beliefs are sometimes ones held by our own family members, particularly older generations.

"And so his eyes lock onto the screen
Being sold someone else's sensational dream
Will he ever escape, will he ever see
And care for reality"

— "Escape" by me

I didn't expect my music lyrics to get such positive feedback. Songs are scary to write and show people, and I wrote that piece based on my real experiences. I really didn't have a lot of time to write it, but I still wanted to put as much thought into it as possible. Any song I'm writing "needs" to be of quality that I'm satisfied with, which is a lot of pressure, but in turn makes me more proud of the work I'm able to do as a musician. This is one of my favorite creative compositions I've made for this class, and I guess it made realize how badly I needed to compose lyrics on this topic.

I hope my posts in this topic allowed people to better recognize this mainstream form of creativity as a way to better connect people, especially to sever the generational divide that's so present in modern families today.

"I would say music has always been a tool for raising awareness, but I think that songs should be addressing social issues more, especially with the current state of the world right now. It is very easy for music to get lost in the entertainment part of the music industry, when in reality what we need right now more than ever is relatability, unity, and understanding."

What's kind of crazy is a lot of people don't really get lyrics, that is, when someone else explains the lyrics to them, they just don't get it. I really do think we get tied up in music sounding good, and while that's fine and all, music has to actually mean something and be effective in communicating it. There's power in the composition, but there's also power in the words. Together, they can connect and unify people. I hope to be able to do this in my songwriting, and I hope artists do so more often as well.

Reproductive & Mental Health

By far one of my favorite and most memorable posts was the one by Vishali. I generally don't like debating too much about reproductive rights, as it sometimes feels like I'm talking "for" women, rather than women voicing their perspectives themselves. In other words, it often feels like it's "not for me to tell." However, it's still a topic that's really important to me, especially considering that Roe v. Wade was overturned (which is bonkers by the way).

"Reproductive access has been under threat and women everywhere are at risk of losing access to necessary care. Professionals in the field of psychology have the responsibility of addressing the social justice issue of reproductive rights and access to equitable resources. It has been found that reproductive autonomy is linked to mental health and well-being (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10199416/). When there are barriers to reproductive healthcare, stigma around certain reproductive procedures, and legislative restrictions, mental well-being decreases and psychological distress increases. It is important to understand how lack of access to comprehensive reproductive care overwhelmingly affects marginalized populations, as they already have less access to physical and mental healthcare (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3540621/)."

— Vishali

I think her letter really effectively addressed a perspective and argument that not many people, including myself, knew about. Mental health is really important to me, as I had to focus on it last year. "Reproductive issues are a psychological issue" is a really strong point, and it impacts me when I think about the women in my life, and how they could also be struggling (and how I could never imagine how they would feel). The letter was proactive and direct with what people can do right now to help women struggling.

I think this letter would also effective if it was read by the general public as well. Lack of reproductive freedom's impact on mental health will definitely be an issue I'll remember and one that I will be sure to bring more awareness to.
