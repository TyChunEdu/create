Being a Writer in My Discipline

Every discipline requires writing skills in some way. Yes, writing is of course important outside any discipline, especially in the world of the internet where media literacy is essential, but there are various ways of communicating through writing. In addition, there are literacy concepts that we must continuously apply to succeed in our discipline.

RLW

Reading like a writer was the very first discussion we had, and just from the discussion alone I had a feeling I would be really excited to contribute to more discussions in the future.

"...the more I think about it, I have subconsciously been RLW for a while..."

— Me

One of the biggest realizations for me was that I had been reading like a (song)writer while listening to music. When someone thinks of reading music, they usually mean reading a music's score or lyrics. What I proposed is that I am able to analyze aspects of the music and take into consideration traditional writing concepts, like diction, intended audience, context, etc. just by listening to it. It's the difference between reading text on a page and hearing text being sung along with the music, while taking in how that lyric fits in with the entire song. Tone and structure are both writing concepts and music concepts.

It was nice to be able to show people the craft of songwriting and composition, and how in-depth it can get beyond whether a song "sounds good" or not. It's a discipline in itself, and I hope discussing it has allowed others to be more creative. Whether it's noting the song's form and storytelling, its ability to provide imagery through diction, its internal vs. external details, its context and complexity, or the vocal choice, it gets people to think, "What if this was different? Is it effective? How would I do it differently? What is this song missing?" This is all parallel to reading like a writer. As I like to say, music is its own language.

I think this was a memorable conversation to have. We often hear songs with very disgusting lyrics, but we overlook them (or "overhear" them I guess) because the composition sounds good. Yes, music often pushes the boundaries of "what's allowed," but only for specific artistic purposes. As I mentioned, I'm more critiquing "lyrics seem tone-deaf, distasteful, or questionable, [where] it can stain the emotions I want to feel." This is well-known concept and critique of music where lyrics that are meant to be creative can negatively impact the quality of the song.

"Hi David,

Do you think it would be a better strategy to write music that captures the audience of many or would it be better to try and find a niche that you can really cater to?

Somewhere in the middle. In my opinion, it's not worth aiming to get the largest audience, as you'll end up disappointing yourself if someone does not like your song. You may also write your song in a less authentic way in order to 'please the masses.' Pretty much every song has an intended audience, and it is rarely just 'the general public.' How large/wide that intended audience is the decision of the artist. That being said, I have to consider how the song would sound to someone that isn't who I'm writing for. There is plenty of music I love that is definitely not meant to be for me. That is usually because I can relate to or understand the song in some way, or I like the aesthetic.

Involving the senses in a song seems like an interesting idea. I can think of many examples with vision and touch and hearing but I can't think of much with taste and smell. Have you written or heard songs that involve those senses? If so, how?

All the time! A song that's in-your-face about it is 'Taste' by Sabrina Carpenter. A more subtle example is 'all-american bitch' by Olivia Rodrigo, 'I'm as fresh as the air.' Because they're rare, they usually have more of an impact on songs, at least from my experience.

I also like the concept of internal and external details that you talked about. Though the song that you are writing sounds pretty heavy. Hope you're doing alright man.

Music is meant to be raw and vulnerable! A common thought is, 'the best songs come from trauma.' Listen to 'ceilings' by Lizzy McAlpine and 'Lucky' by Halsey. I appreciate you being worried though!

The 'lyrics are not songs without music' made me think. A lot of poetry sounds like lyrics without music. Do you think they could be made into songs by adding music to it?

Poetry and music lyrics can be similar, although there are notable differences. Songs have a certain musical flow (e.g. rhythm) and structure (form) that poems do not. They're meant to be read (and processed as they're reading). They probably could be made into songs given the right artist, and it's been done before, but some poems would definitely be incredibly difficult to make musical.

T.C."

(Me responding to David's questions in response to my post)

UTL

"I think my recent examples of unlearning to learn are more personal, and they all have to do with confidence in myself.

Reflecting upon my time in college as my final semester comes to an end, it is so easy to have regrets. Classic 'post-college blues.' I have made huge mistakes during these 4 years that definitely in my perspective 'set me back,' so much so that they have overshadowed the many great accomplishments I have had here. These mistakes set my self-confidence to an all time low, and led me to distrust myself. These last couple years have been significant for my personal development where I had to unlearn these negative beliefs about myself and allow myself to grow in the future. There are a number of things I wish I did during college, but I needed to realize that I am in a good place and where I need to be, right here, right now. Maybe the unreached goals I set for myself do not have to end after college. I think this was all a lot to take in, since life can sometimes feel like there's constantly an hourglass with sand going down the tube.

I want to discuss Unlearning to Learn by Gupte. I totally forgot that Yoda taught this concept! Star Wars aside, I found the write-up very profound. In a world of rapid change, we are always slow to adapt to it. I understand that it is difficult for people to change as they get older and issues become more complex, but we are failing to conquer biases and unlearn outdated understandings. It often feels like we're going backwards, making our world more like how it was hundreds of years ago because we do not want to change and have forgotten how important the progress we have made so far is. We take everything that we do not physically see or pay attention to for granted, not realizing that it took time and effort to get ourselves to where we are today, so much so that we are willing to go back, as the article says, to where we have always done it. We let fear conquer us, rather than unlearn the past and learn to adapt to the future. Maybe we focus so much on changing the world 'with our bare hands,' and completely disregard the idea that we need to change."

— Me

I took interesting approach to giving an example of unlearning to learn. It is difficult to really discuss it, since it is personal and touches on the not so great parts of my life. How do I be specific, despite the very personal details I would have to reveal? You could guess what my solution is. Songwriting has its way of allowing artists to be very specific with their experiences. Not only do those experiences become a part of an art piece, but they allow people to connect to them better. Music emphasizes that everyone goes through similar struggles. Even if the super-specific details are left out, generally people gain a good enough understanding of that experience, perhaps through the music composition which illustrates the artist's emotions.

I don't think I realized that people had actually replied to my post. I don't think I checked the discussion since it was a reflection post. Eshani and Thomas asked if my change in perspective had affected my hope for future and my aspirations. It absolutely has. I now have the mindset of "maybe it doesn't have to end just yet." I was able to really open my eyes to opportunities outside college, and how exciting the future could be. I have a lot of goals for myself, and I have gained confidence in myself that I will achieve them.

My last paragraph in this post was hinting at social issues. The article immediately made me think of how not being able to unlearn to learn has worsened social justice. I think our society's resistance to change irked me enough that I just had to write that paragraph.
